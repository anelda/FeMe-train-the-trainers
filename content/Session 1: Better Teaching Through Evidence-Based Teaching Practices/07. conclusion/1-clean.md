### Pulling it all together

This session covered a lot of ground. Let's step back and see how the pieces connect — because they do connect, and that matters for the way you design your training.

You started by making your own assumptions about teaching visible. That matters because assumptions shape every decision you make as a trainer, often without you noticing. The rest of the session gave you evidence-based ideas to test those assumptions against.

From there, you explored how people actually learn. Working memory is limited, understanding beats memorisation, and learners are often worse judges of their own progress than they think. Techniques like retrieval practice, spaced repetition, and chunking aren't tricks — they're responses to how memory genuinely works. When you design training that accounts for these realities, your learners retain more and understand more deeply.

You then looked at two sets of mindsets. As an educator, you saw that teaching is itself a skill that improves with practice and feedback — and that the curse of knowledge can quietly undermine even your best explanations. For your learners, you explored how motivation, psychological safety, and productive struggle shape whether people engage or withdraw. The takeaway: the environment you create matters as much as the content you deliver.

In the section on designing content, you worked through backward design — starting from what learners should be able to *do*, then building assessments and activities to get them there. Constructive alignment, scaffolding, and sequencing all serve the same purpose: making sure every part of your training pulls in the same direction.

Finally, you looked at assessment not as a verdict, but as a tool. Frequent, low-stakes checks tell you what's working and what isn't — for your learners and for you. They also double as powerful learning activities in their own right.
