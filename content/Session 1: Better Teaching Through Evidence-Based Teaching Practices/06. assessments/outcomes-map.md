# Outcomes Map

## Outcomes → Exercises

### Outcome 1: Design assessments that reveal where learners are struggling, rather than simply ranking them.
- **Bloom's level:** Create (verb: *design*)
- **Exercise:** What would you change? — Bloom's level: Analyse (learners evaluate their current method and identify what it misses)
- **Exercise:** Map your assessment points — Bloom's level: Create (learners design a timeline with new assessment checkpoints)
- **Exercise:** Three non-test assessments — Bloom's level: Create (learners design three alternative assessment approaches)

### Outcome 2: Distinguish between signal and noise in assessment results, and adjust your design to reduce noise.
- **Bloom's level:** Analyse (verb: *distinguish*) + Apply (verb: *adjust*) — highest: Analyse
- **Exercise:** Separate signal from noise — Bloom's level: Analyse (learners identify noise sources and propose a change to reduce noise)

### Outcome 3: Explain why frequent, low-stakes assessment supports learning more effectively than a single high-stakes exam.
- **Bloom's level:** Understand (verb: *explain*)
- **Exercise:** Map your assessment points — Bloom's level: Apply (learners apply the principle by adding low-stakes checkpoints to their own timeline)

### Outcome 4: Recognise the difference between recall and recognition, and choose assessment formats accordingly.
- **Bloom's level:** Understand (verb: *recognise*) + Apply (verb: *choose*) — highest: Apply
- **Exercise:** Recognition vs. recall — Bloom's level: Evaluate (learners compare two formats and judge which to use and why)

### Outcome 5: Identify how test anxiety affects learners and apply practical strategies to reduce it.
- **Bloom's level:** Remember (verb: *identify*) + Apply (verb: *apply*) — highest: Apply
- **Exercise:** Write your assessment introduction — Bloom's level: Create (learners draft an original explanation to reduce anxiety)

## Bloom's Alignment Issues

### Outcome 1 (Create) ↔ What would you change? (Analyse)
- **⚠️ EXERCISE UNDERSHOOTS OUTCOME** — The exercise asks learners to analyse their current method, but the outcome requires them to *design* new assessments. However, this is an early scaffolding exercise that feeds into later design activities, so this is a deliberate progression rather than a true gap.

### Outcome 3 (Understand) ↔ Map your assessment points (Apply)
- **⚠️ EXERCISE EXCEEDS OUTCOME** — The exercise asks learners to *apply* the spacing principle to their own timeline, but the outcome only asks them to *explain* why frequent assessment works. Suggestion: level up the outcome verb from "explain" to "apply" or "plan" — which better matches what the exercise actually asks them to do.

### Outcome 4 (Apply) ↔ Recognition vs. recall (Evaluate)
- **⚠️ EXERCISE EXCEEDS OUTCOME** — The exercise asks learners to *evaluate* and *justify* their format choice, but the outcome only asks them to *recognise* and *choose*. Suggestion: level up the outcome verb to include "evaluate" or "justify", or accept that the exercise deliberately stretches beyond the outcome.

### Outcome 5 (Apply) ↔ Write your assessment introduction (Create)
- **⚠️ EXERCISE EXCEEDS OUTCOME** — The exercise asks learners to *create* an original explanation, but the outcome only asks them to *identify* and *apply*. Suggestion: level up the outcome to include "design" or "draft", or accept that creating a short text is a natural extension of applying the strategy.

## Flags for Review

### Bloom's level mismatches

1. **Outcome 3 undershoots its exercise.** The outcome says "explain" (Understand) but the exercise "Map your assessment points" asks learners to *apply* the principle. **Suggestion:** Change the outcome to something like: *"Plan frequent, low-stakes assessment points across a training programme, based on evidence that spacing supports learning more effectively than a single high-stakes exam."* This makes the outcome match the Apply level of the exercise.

2. **Outcome 4 undershoots its exercise.** The outcome says "recognise... and choose" (Apply) but the exercise "Recognition vs. recall" asks learners to *evaluate* and *justify* which format to use. **Suggestion:** Change the outcome to: *"Evaluate the difference between recall and recognition, and justify your choice of assessment format."* Or accept the stretch and leave it — the mismatch is mild.

3. **Outcome 5 undershoots its exercise.** The outcome says "identify... and apply" (Apply) but the exercise asks learners to *create* an original explanation. **Suggestion:** Change the outcome to: *"Draft a transparent assessment introduction that reduces test anxiety, drawing on evidence about how anxiety affects learner performance."* Or keep the outcome and accept that the exercise deliberately stretches beyond it.

### Weak links

4. **Outcome 3 has only indirect exercise coverage.** "Map your assessment points" addresses spacing but never asks the learner to *explain* why frequent assessment works — they just do it. If the outcome stays at the Understand level, there is no exercise that tests whether the learner can actually articulate the reasoning. **Suggestion:** If you level up the outcome to Apply (flag 1), this resolves itself. If you keep "explain", consider adding a brief prompt: *"In one or two sentences, explain to a colleague why you spread assessment across the timeline rather than concentrating it at the end."*

### No unmet outcomes, no orphan exercises, no vague verbs

- All five outcomes are covered by at least one exercise.
- All six exercises serve at least one outcome.
- All outcome verbs are specific Bloom's-aligned verbs (design, distinguish, adjust, explain, recognise, choose, identify, apply).
