Before we dive into the content of this workbook, take a moment to reflect. Grab a pen and paper (or open a notebook) and write your answers to these questions:

- What does learning mean to you? Think about yourself as a learner.
- How do you want your learners to experience learning in your course? Think about yourself as a teacher.
- What teaching methods will you use in your course?
- What will your learners be able to do — or what behavioural change would you like to see in them?

### Why we start with reflection

Everyone already holds beliefs about what learning is and how it happens. Those beliefs shape how we design our teaching — often without us realising it. By pausing to put your ideas into words, you make your assumptions visible. That matters because it lets you engage more critically with everything that follows.

We are asking these questions to help you:

- Surface the assumptions that shape how you design your teaching.
- Clarify what kind of change you are trying to produce in your learners.
- Examine whether your methods align with your goals.

This reflection creates a foundation for the rest of the course. It gives you a baseline to compare against the evidence about how people learn, and helps you spot where you might want to adjust or strengthen your approach.

### A tool you can reuse

You can use this same approach in your own workshops or courses. Starting a session with reflection helps participants connect new content to their own context, surfaces misconceptions early, and models active learning from the outset. It turns information delivery into intentional design.
