# Outcomes Map

## Outcomes → Exercises

### Outcome 1: Identify the assumptions you hold about learning and how they influence your teaching choices.
- **Bloom's level:** Remember (verb: identify)
- **Exercise:** Opening reflection (lines 17–22) — Bloom's level: Apply (learners apply reflective thinking to their own context to surface assumptions)

### Outcome 2: Distinguish between intuitive beliefs about teaching and evidence-informed approaches.
- **Bloom's level:** Analyse (verb: distinguish)
- **⚠️ UNMET** — no exercise directly addresses this outcome. The prose (lines 26–34) discusses why assumptions matter, but no exercise asks learners to actively distinguish intuitive beliefs from evidence-informed ones.

### Outcome 3: Describe how structured reflection can surface gaps between your goals and your methods.
- **Bloom's level:** Understand (verb: describe)
- **Exercise:** Opening reflection (lines 17–22) — Bloom's level: Apply — **weak link**. Learners *experience* reflection surfacing gaps, but the exercise does not ask them to *describe* the mechanism. They do it; they are not asked to articulate how or why it works.

### Outcome 4: Explain how starting with reflection models active learning for your own participants.
- **Bloom's level:** Understand (verb: explain)
- **⚠️ UNMET** — no exercise asks learners to explain this. The prose in "A tool you can reuse" (lines 36–38) explains it *to* the learner, but the learner is never asked to explain it themselves.

### Outcome 5: Design a simple reflective prompt you could use at the start of your own training session.
- **Bloom's level:** Create (verb: design)
- **Exercise:** Write your own opening reflection (lines 40–42) — Bloom's level: Create (learners produce original reflective questions)

## Bloom's Alignment Issues

### Outcome 1 × Opening reflection
- **⚠️ EXERCISE EXCEEDS OUTCOME** — The outcome verb is "identify" (Remember), but the exercise asks learners to *apply* reflective thinking to their own context and articulate their beliefs, which operates at the Apply level. Suggestion: level up the outcome verb to "articulate" or "examine" (Analyse), which better reflects what the exercise actually demands.

### Outcome 3 × Opening reflection
- **⚠️ EXERCISE UNDERSHOOTS OUTCOME** (indirect) — The outcome asks learners to *describe* the mechanism (Understand), but the exercise only has them *do* the reflection (Apply). They are not asked to step back and describe *how* reflection surfaces gaps. Suggestion: add a brief follow-up prompt after the reflection (e.g., "Look at your answers. Did anything surprise you? What gap, if any, did you notice between your goals and your methods?") or lower the outcome to "Experience how structured reflection can surface gaps."

## Flags for Review

### Unmet outcomes
1. **Outcome 2 (Distinguish intuitive beliefs from evidence-informed approaches)** — This is an Analyse-level outcome with no supporting exercise. Options:
   - **Add a short exercise** after the "Why we start with reflection" section: e.g., "Look at your answers to the reflection above. Which of your beliefs come from personal experience or intuition? Which are informed by evidence or research? Mark them."
   - **Defer to a later section** if the distinction between intuitive and evidence-based approaches is covered more fully elsewhere in the workbook (e.g., in "How People Learn"). If so, remove this outcome from this section.
   - **Relax the outcome** to Understand level: "Recognise that teaching choices are often shaped by intuition rather than evidence."

2. **Outcome 4 (Explain how reflection models active learning)** — This is an Understand-level outcome with no exercise. The prose explains it, but learners never practise explaining it. Options:
   - **Add a brief exercise** after "A tool you can reuse": e.g., "In one or two sentences, explain to a colleague why you might open a training session with reflection rather than a lecture."
   - **Fold it into the existing activity** (lines 40–42) by adding: "Briefly note *why* you chose to open with reflection — what does it model for participants?"
   - **Remove the outcome** if the goal is just that learners *read* the rationale, not that they can explain it independently.

### Weak links
3. **Outcome 3 × Opening reflection** — Learners experience reflection but are never asked to describe the mechanism. Options:
   - Add a follow-up prompt: "Now look back at what you wrote. Did reflection reveal any gap between what you want your learners to experience and the methods you planned to use?"
   - Lower the outcome: "Experience how structured reflection can surface gaps between your goals and your methods."

### Bloom's mismatches
4. **Outcome 1 (Remember: "identify") × Opening reflection (Apply)** — The exercise is richer than the outcome suggests. Options:
   - **Level up the outcome** to: "Examine the assumptions you hold about learning and how they influence your teaching choices" (Analyse) or "Articulate the assumptions you hold..." (Apply/Understand).
   - Leave as-is if you want a low-bar outcome that the exercise exceeds (acceptable, but the outcome undersells what learners actually do).

### Vague verbs
- No outcome verbs are outright vague — all use recognisable Bloom's verbs. However, "identify" in Outcome 1 is at the Remember level, which undersells the cognitive work the exercise demands. Suggested replacement: **"examine"** (Analyse) or **"articulate"** (Understand/Apply).
