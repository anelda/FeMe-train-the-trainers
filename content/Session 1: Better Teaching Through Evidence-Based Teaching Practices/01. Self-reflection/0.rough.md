Before we start with the content of this workbook, we would like to encourage you to consider the following questions. Write the answers to these questions down on paper or in a notebook.

What does learning mean to you? Think about yourself as a learner
How do you want your learners to experience learning in your course? Think about yourself as a teacher.
What teaching methods will I use in my course? 
What will your learners be able to do, or what behavioural change would you like to see in them?

We begin with self-reflection because everyone already holds beliefs about what learning is and how it happens. Those beliefs shape how we design teaching — often without us realising it. By pausing to articulate what we value, what we expect students to experience, and what changes we hope to see, we make our assumptions visible. This helps us engage more critically with the ideas that follow.

You can use this same approach in your own workshops or courses. Starting with reflection helps participants connect new content to their own context, surfaces misconceptions early, and models active learning from the outset. It turns the session from information delivery into intentional design.

By asking you these questions, we aim to make your current thinking about learning and teaching visible — to yourself and to others.

We want to surface the assumptions that shape how you design your teaching, clarify what kind of change you are trying to produce in your learners, and examine whether your methods align with your goals.

This reflection creates a foundation for the rest of the course. It allows you to compare your current beliefs with evidence about how people learn, and to identify where you may want to adjust or strengthen your approach.
