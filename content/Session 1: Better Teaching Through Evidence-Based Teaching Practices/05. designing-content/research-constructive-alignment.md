# Research: Constructive Alignment

## Summary of Findings

Constructive alignment is a well-established and widely recognised concept in higher education and instructional design. It was originated by **Professor John B. Biggs**, first formally published in 1996 and further developed in his influential book *Teaching for Quality Learning at University* (co-authored with Catherine Tang from the 3rd edition onward).

The concept combines two ideas:

1. **Constructivism** -- the principle that learners construct meaning through their own activity (not by passively receiving information).
2. **Alignment** -- the principle that intended learning outcomes, teaching/learning activities, and assessment tasks should all be deliberately designed to support each other.

In Biggs's own framing: you start by defining the intended learning outcomes (ILOs), then design teaching and learning activities that are likely to elicit those outcomes, and finally design assessment tasks that directly measure whether the outcomes have been achieved. All three components must be aligned.

## Verification of Workbook Claims

### Claim 1: "Constructive alignment is the idea that your objectives, activities, and assessments should all reinforce each other"

**Verdict: Accurate.** This is a fair plain-language summary. Biggs's formal definition states that constructive alignment involves aligning curriculum objectives, teaching/learning activities, and assessment of student performance. The workbook's use of "reinforce each other" captures the spirit of mutual alignment well.

### Claim 2: It is a named, established concept in education

**Verdict: Correct.** Constructive alignment is a named, widely cited concept. The term was coined by John Biggs, first appearing in his 1996 journal article and subsequently developed across multiple editions of his textbook. It has its own Wikipedia article and is referenced in institutional teaching guidance across universities worldwide (e.g., Queen Mary University of London, Advance HE, University of Helsinki).

## Historical Note

Biggs notes that the alignment aspect of his framework builds on ideas from Ralph W. Tyler's *Basic Principles of Curriculum and Instruction* (1949), which emphasised that objectives, learning experiences, and evaluation should be coherently linked. Biggs's contribution was to ground this alignment principle explicitly in constructivist learning theory.
