# Designing Content

Questions:
- When you last designed a training session, did you start with the content or the outcomes?
- Have you ever sat through a course that tried to cover too much and ended up teaching too little?

## Start with the end in mind

It is tempting to dive straight into creating slides, writing notes, or choosing activities. But effective training design works backwards. This approach — often called backward design — means you start by defining what your learners should know or be able to do by the end, then figure out how you will know they have got there, and only then choose the content and activities that will get them to that point.

This matters because training without clear goals tends to drift. You add a topic because it is interesting, then another because it feels related, and before long you have a packed schedule that covers a lot but teaches very little. Clear learning objectives act as a filter: if something does not serve an objective, it probably does not belong.

Think of your training as a set of modules, each one focused on a coherent piece of the bigger picture. Every module should have its own objectives, and those objectives should connect logically to the overall goals of the programme. This modularity also makes your content easier to update, rearrange, or share with other trainers.

### How activities and assessments connect to objectives

Once you have your objectives, every activity and every assessment should map back to at least one of them. If an objective says learners will be able to "apply a framework to a new scenario", then a lecture alone will not get them there — they need a hands-on task. If the assessment is a multiple-choice quiz, it is testing recognition, not application. We cover assessments in detail in the next section, but the key idea here is alignment: objectives, activities, and assessments should all point in the same direction.

This alignment also helps prevent scope creep. When someone suggests adding a topic, you can ask a simple question: which objective does this serve? If the answer is "none of them", you have your answer.

## Writing strong learning objectives

A learning objective should describe something observable and measurable. "Understand cognitive load" is vague — how would you know if someone understands it? Compare that with "explain how cognitive load affects learning in a short training session" or "redesign a slide deck to reduce unnecessary cognitive load". These are concrete. You can watch someone do them, and you can tell whether they have done them well.

Bloom's taxonomy offers a useful way to think about the depth of thinking you are asking for. It arranges cognitive skills in a hierarchy: remember, understand, apply, analyse, evaluate, create. Each level builds on the ones below it. When you write objectives, choose verbs that match the level of thinking that actually matters for your learners. If they need to apply a skill in their own context, an objective pitched at "remember" or "define" is not ambitious enough.

Here are a few examples of weak objectives rewritten as strong ones:

- **Weak:** "Understand feedback techniques." **Strong:** "Give constructive feedback on a peer's lesson plan using a provided rubric."
- **Weak:** "Know about open educational resources." **Strong:** "Evaluate an existing OER for quality and relevance to a specific training objective."
- **Weak:** "Learn about scaffolding." **Strong:** "Sequence a set of activities so that each one builds on the skills practised in the previous one."

## Constructive alignment

Constructive alignment is the idea that your objectives, activities, and assessments should all reinforce each other. When they are aligned, everything in your training is pulling in the same direction. When they are not, learners get mixed signals about what matters.

Here is a simple example. Suppose your objective is: "Learners will be able to apply the backward design framework to plan a short training session."

- **Aligned activity:** Learners work in pairs to draft a session plan starting from objectives, then designing an assessment, then choosing activities.
- **Aligned assessment:** Each pair presents their plan and explains how their activities connect to their objectives.
- **Misaligned activity:** A forty-minute lecture on the history of backward design.
- **Partial assessment:** A quiz asking learners to define "backward design" in their own words.

The lecture and the quiz are not wrong — checking that learners can explain a concept is often a reasonable stepping stone toward applying it. But on their own, they do not go far enough. If the objective asks learners to apply the framework, they eventually need an activity and an assessment that give them a chance to do exactly that. A definition quiz might confirm understanding, but it does not tell you whether someone can actually use the framework to plan a session.

This idea connects directly to the assessment section that follows. There we go deeper into how to design assessments that genuinely test what your objectives claim to teach.

## Sequencing and scaffolding

The order in which you teach things matters more than most people realise. If you introduce a concept before learners have the foundation to make sense of it, they will struggle — not because the material is too hard, but because it arrived too early.

Good sequencing generally means building from simpler to more complex, making sure prerequisite knowledge is in place before it is needed. But that is not the only approach that works. Sometimes it is useful to throw learners at a challenge that is deliberately beyond their current reach, let them struggle with it, and then circle back to fill in the gaps they just discovered. This can be highly motivating — the struggle creates a felt need for the knowledge that follows, so learners engage with it more deeply than they would if it had arrived as an abstract prerequisite.

Either way, the underlying principle is the same. This connects back to the zone of proximal development covered in the "How People Learn" section: learners grow when they are stretched just beyond what they can already do. The difference is in how you reveal that stretch — gradually through careful sequencing, or deliberately through a well-chosen challenge that exposes what they still need to learn.

Scaffolding is the practical side of this. It means providing temporary support structures that help learners bridge the gap between what they know and what you are asking them to do. A worked example before an independent task is scaffolding. A simplified version of a problem before the full one is scaffolding. A quick recap of yesterday's key ideas before introducing today's new concept is scaffolding. As learners build competence, you gradually remove those supports.

### Practical steps for sequencing your content

Map out the topics you want to cover and identify the dependencies between them. Which concepts rely on others? What needs to come first? Where might learners need a bridge — a recap, a warm-up exercise, or a simpler version of a task — before they are ready for the next step? Drawing a simple dependency map, even on the back of a napkin, can save you from discovering mid-session that your learners are lost because you skipped a building block.

## Pitching content at the right level

Your readers will be designing training for varied audiences, so pitching content at the right level is a skill worth developing deliberately. Pitch too low and capable learners disengage. Pitch too high and struggling learners shut down.

The starting point is finding out what your learners already know. A quick needs assessment before you design — even something as simple as a short survey or a few conversation questions — gives you a realistic picture instead of assumptions. You do not need a formal diagnostic test; you need enough information to make informed design choices.

Once you know your audience, consider building in flexibility. Optional "deep dive" sections let advanced learners go further without holding everyone else back. Being explicit about what prior knowledge you are assuming — stating it at the start of a module — helps learners self-assess whether they are ready, and helps you avoid the awkward moment where half the room is confused and the other half is bored.

## Less is more

One of the hardest lessons in training design is that cutting content often improves learning. This goes beyond avoiding scope creep. Cognitive load theory, covered in the "How People Learn" section, explains why: working memory is limited, and every additional piece of information competes for attention. If something does not directly serve a learning objective, it is a candidate for removal — even if it is genuinely interesting.

This does not mean your training should be thin. It means it should be focused. A session that covers three concepts well, with time for practice and questions, will produce more learning than one that races through ten concepts with no time to absorb any of them.

Get into the habit of asking "what can I remove?" as often as you ask "what should I add?" A focused, well-paced session beats an exhaustive one every time.

## Using and adapting existing content

Not everything needs to be written from scratch. Open educational resources (OER) — freely available teaching materials that you can use, adapt, and redistribute — are a practical starting point for many topics. Published tutorials, videos, case studies, and other materials can save you significant design time while giving your learners exposure to different voices and perspectives.

This fits the philosophy of this workbook: open training benefits from shared, reusable resources. You do not need to reinvent every explanation or create every diagram yourself.

### Finding and evaluating resources

When looking for existing content, consider repositories like OER Commons, MIT OpenCourseWare, or discipline-specific collections in your field. For any resource you find, ask yourself:

- Does it align with my learning objectives?
- Is it pitched at the right level for my learners?
- Is it accurate and up to date?
- Can I adapt it to fit my context, or does it only work as-is?

When you do use or adapt someone else's work, credit them properly. This is both an ethical obligation and a practical one — it models good practice for your own learners and contributes to the culture of open sharing that makes OER possible.

## Common pitfalls

Even with clear objectives and good intentions, certain mistakes show up repeatedly in training design. Watching for these can save you from learning them the hard way.

**Designing around your interests rather than the objectives.** Your passion for a subtopic is not a reason to include it. Every piece of content should earn its place by serving a learning objective, not by being something you find fascinating.

**Confusing coverage with learning.** Covering a topic is not the same as learners actually learning it. If you have "covered" something but learners cannot do anything with it afterwards, the coverage was not useful. Depth beats breadth.

**Front-loading theory before practice.** Learners lose momentum when they sit through extended theoretical input before getting to do anything with it. They struggle to connect abstract ideas to anything concrete because they have no experience to anchor them to yet. Interleave theory and practice wherever you can.

**Designing in isolation.** Getting feedback on your content from a colleague or a sample learner before you deliver it catches problems you cannot see yourself. A fresh pair of eyes will spot assumptions you did not realise you were making.

**Assuming one format fits all.** A wall of text works for some topics, but not all. Mix in visuals, examples, discussions, and hands-on tasks where they serve the objectives. Variety is not decoration — it is a response to the reality that different ideas are best communicated in different ways.
