# Research: Backward Design

## Summary of findings

**Backward design** is a well-established and widely recognised approach in instructional design and curriculum development. It was popularised by **Grant Wiggins** and **Jay McTighe** in their 1998 book *Understanding by Design*, with an expanded second edition published in 2005. The approach is also referred to as the **Understanding by Design (UbD) framework**.

The concept has deeper historical roots: it builds on ideas from **Ralph W. Tyler**, whose 1949 book *Basic Principles of Curriculum and Instruction* argued that curriculum planning should begin with clear objectives. Wiggins and McTighe formalised and named the "backward design" process, making it one of the most cited frameworks in modern instructional design.

## Claim verification

### Claim 1: Backward design means "you start by defining what your learners should know or be able to do by the end, then figure out how you will know they have got there, and only then choose the content and activities"

**Verdict: Accurate.**

This is a faithful plain-language summary of the three stages of backward design as described by Wiggins and McTighe:

| Stage | Wiggins & McTighe's label | What it involves |
|-------|--------------------------|------------------|
| 1 | **Identify desired results** | Define what learners should know, understand, and be able to do. |
| 2 | **Determine acceptable evidence** | Decide how you will know learners have achieved those results (assessment). |
| 3 | **Plan learning experiences and instruction** | Choose content, activities, and teaching strategies that support the desired results. |

The workbook's description captures all three stages in the correct order and with the correct emphasis: outcomes first, then evidence of learning, then activities and content.

### Claim 2: It is a well-known, named approach in instructional design

**Verdict: Accurate.**

Backward design / Understanding by Design is one of the most widely referenced curriculum design frameworks in education. It is used across K-12, higher education, and professional training contexts. It appears in institutional teaching guides from universities worldwide (e.g., University of Illinois Chicago, University of Central Florida, Western Michigan University) and is a standard topic in instructional design literature.
