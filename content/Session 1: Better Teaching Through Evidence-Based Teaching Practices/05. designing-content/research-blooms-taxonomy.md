# Research: Bloom's Taxonomy

## Summary of findings

**Bloom's taxonomy** is one of the most widely used frameworks for classifying educational learning objectives. The **original taxonomy** was published in 1956 by Benjamin Bloom and collaborators. It defined six levels of cognitive complexity using noun forms: **Knowledge, Comprehension, Application, Analysis, Synthesis, and Evaluation**.

In 2001, a group led by **Lorin Anderson** (a former student of Bloom) and **David Krathwohl** (one of the original taxonomy's co-authors) published a major revision. The **revised taxonomy** renamed the six levels using verb forms to emphasise active cognitive processes: **Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyse, Evaluate, and Create**. The revision also reordered the top two levels -- Synthesis (renamed Create) moved above Evaluation (renamed Evaluate) -- on the grounds that producing something original requires evaluative judgment, making creation the more cognitively demanding task.

The revised taxonomy also introduced a second dimension: the **Knowledge Dimension**, with four types of knowledge (Factual, Conceptual, Procedural, and Metacognitive). This made the taxonomy a two-dimensional framework rather than a simple linear hierarchy.

## Claim verification

### Claim 1: Bloom's taxonomy "arranges cognitive skills in a hierarchy: remember, understand, apply, analyse, evaluate, create"

**Verdict: Accurate, but should be attributed to the revised taxonomy.**

The six levels listed -- remember, understand, apply, analyse, evaluate, create -- are correct and in the correct order. These are the levels of the **revised Bloom's taxonomy** (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001), not the original 1956 version. The use of verb forms (rather than nouns) is the clearest marker of the revised version.

For reference, here is the comparison:

| Level | Original (Bloom, 1956) | Revised (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) |
|-------|----------------------|--------------------------------------|
| 1 (lowest) | Knowledge | **Remember** |
| 2 | Comprehension | **Understand** |
| 3 | Application | **Apply** |
| 4 | Analysis | **Analyse** |
| 5 | Synthesis | **Evaluate** |
| 6 (highest) | Evaluation | **Create** |

Note that the top two levels swapped positions in the revision: Evaluation was originally the highest level, but Create (formerly Synthesis) became the highest in the revised version.

The workbook is describing the **revised version** without explicitly labelling it as such. This is common practice in education -- the revised version has largely superseded the original in everyday use -- but it may be worth noting that this is the "revised" taxonomy for precision.

### Claim 2: "Each level builds on the ones below it"

**Verdict: Broadly accepted but an oversimplification.**

This claim reflects the **cumulative hierarchy** model that was central to the original 1956 taxonomy. The idea was that mastery of lower levels (e.g., remembering facts) was a prerequisite for performing higher-level tasks (e.g., analysis or evaluation).

However, research has challenged this as an oversimplification:

1. **The revised taxonomy itself adopts a "relaxed hierarchy"** rather than a strict cumulative one. Krathwohl (2002) describes the cognitive-process dimension as organised from simple to complex, but acknowledges that the strict cumulative hierarchy of the original is not fully supported.

2. **Empirical research does not support a strict hierarchy.** Larsen et al. (2022) found that direct connections exist between non-consecutive categories (e.g., a student can analyse without having fully mastered application). Their study found "empirical evidence for a relaxed hierarchy within the cognitive-process dimension, with overlap occurring among nonadjacent levels."

3. **Cognitive processes are interconnected, not discrete.** Real-world learning tasks often engage multiple cognitive levels simultaneously. For example, creating something new may involve remembering, understanding, and evaluating all at once -- not in strict sequence.

4. **The original authors knew about this limitation.** Even in the 1956 publication, Bloom and colleagues noted that the hierarchical ordering was a working hypothesis. Subsequent empirical work (particularly from the 1960s-1980s) provided only partial support for the strict ordering.

**Recommendation for the workbook:** The claim is a useful simplification for introducing the framework to newcomers, but it would be more accurate to say something like: "The levels are generally arranged from simpler to more complex cognitive processes, though in practice they overlap and interact rather than forming a strict step-by-step sequence."
