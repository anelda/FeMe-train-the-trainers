Writing good learning objectives — use actionable, measurable verbs rather than vague ones. "Understand X" is hard to measure; "explain X", "apply X to a new scenario", "compare X and Y" are concrete and observable. Bloom's taxonomy provides a useful hierarchy here (remember → understand → apply → analyse → evaluate → create). Encourage readers to aim for the level of thinking that actually matters for their learners, not just recall. Give examples of weak vs strong objectives side by side.

Constructive alignment — objectives, activities, and assessments should all point in the same direction. If an objective says "apply X" but the activity is a lecture and the assessment is a quiz, things are misaligned. Walk through a simple example: pick an objective, show what an aligned activity looks like, and what an aligned assessment looks like. This is the conceptual thread that connects this section to the assessments section (08) — name the connection but don't duplicate the detail.

Sequencing and scaffolding — deciding what order to teach things in matters. Build from simpler to more complex, make sure prerequisite knowledge is covered before it's needed. This connects back to "zone of proximal development" from section 03 — learners need to be stretched just beyond what they can already do, not thrown into the deep end. Practical advice: map out dependencies between topics, identify what needs to come first, and flag where learners might need a bridge (a quick recap, a worked example, a simpler version of the task before the full one).

Aligning content depth to your audience — how to pitch material at the right level for the people in the room. Your readers are designing training for varied audiences, so they need to think about what their learners already know and what they don't. Too basic and people disengage, too advanced and they're lost. Practical strategies: do a quick needs assessment before designing, consider creating optional "deep dive" sections for advanced learners, and be explicit about what prior knowledge you're assuming.

The "less is more" principle — cutting content often improves learning. This goes beyond just avoiding scope creep. Cognitive load (covered in section 03) explains why: working memory is limited, and every extra piece of information competes for attention. If something doesn't directly serve a learning objective, it's a candidate for cutting — even if it's interesting. Encourage readers to ask "what can I remove?" as often as they ask "what should I add?" A focused, well-paced session beats an exhaustive one.

Selecting and curating content vs creating from scratch — not everything needs to be written from scratch. Encourage trainers to look for existing open educational resources (OER), published tutorials, videos, case studies, and other materials they can adapt or incorporate. This saves time, often gives learners exposure to different voices and perspectives, and fits the "open training" philosophy of this workbook. Practical guidance: where to find OER, how to evaluate quality, how to adapt existing materials to fit your objectives, and how to credit sources properly.

Common pitfalls when designing content:
- Writing content you find interesting rather than what serves the objectives — your passion for a subtopic isn't a reason to include it
- Confusing coverage with learning — covering a topic is not the same as learners actually learning it; depth beats breadth
- Front-loading too much theory before any practice — learners lose momentum and can't connect abstract ideas to anything concrete yet
- Designing in isolation — not getting feedback on your content from a colleague or a sample learner before delivering it
- Assuming one format fits all — a wall of text works for some topics but not others; mix in visuals, examples, discussions, and hands-on tasks where they serve the objectives
