# Workshop Activities: Designing Content

These activities are designed for a facilitator running a live workshop based on this chapter. Each activity includes timing, group size, materials needed, and step-by-step instructions.

## Activity 1: Backward Design Speed Round

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Pairs
**Materials:** One index card or sticky note per person, pens

### Instructions

1. Ask each participant to think of a training session they have delivered or are planning. Give them two minutes to write down a single sentence on their card: *what should learners be able to do by the end of that session?*
2. Pairs swap cards. Each person now has their partner's outcome statement. Working alone for five minutes, they jot down on the back of the card: (a) one way to check whether learners achieved that outcome, and (b) one activity that would help learners get there.
3. Pairs discuss for five minutes. The original author explains their outcome; the partner shares the assessment and activity they designed for it. Together, they compare: does the partner's plan match what the author had in mind? Where does it diverge?
4. Debrief as a whole group (five minutes). Ask two or three pairs to share what surprised them. Prompt with: "Did your partner interpret your outcome the way you intended? If not, what was ambiguous?"

### Facilitator notes

The point of the swap is to test whether the outcome statement is clear enough that someone else can design to it. If partners consistently misinterpret the intent, that is a signal the objective needs sharper language — which sets up the next section on writing strong objectives. Keep the energy up by enforcing the two-minute writing limit strictly.

## Activity 2: The Verb Upgrade

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** One printed handout per group with 5–6 weak learning objectives (prepare these in advance, or use the examples below)

### Instructions

1. Hand out the sheet of weak objectives. If you do not have a printed handout, display the following on a projector and ask groups to copy them down:
   - "Understand data management principles."
   - "Learn about open licensing."
   - "Be familiar with qualitative research methods."
   - "Know the basics of facilitation."
   - "Appreciate the value of peer review."
2. Each group has eight minutes to rewrite every objective so that it is observable and measurable. Encourage them to think about what a learner would actually *do* to demonstrate the skill — and to pitch the verb at the right level of thinking for the context they imagine.
3. Groups read out their strongest rewrite. After each one, ask the rest of the room: "Could you watch someone do this? Would you know whether they did it well?" If the answer is no, invite a quick suggestion to tighten it further.

### Facilitator notes

Groups often default to "explain" or "describe" for everything. If you notice this, nudge them: "Those are fine verbs, but are they always the right level? If you want learners to actually *use* a skill, what verb would capture that?" You do not need to teach Bloom's taxonomy formally here — the activity builds intuition for it. If participants ask about the taxonomy, point them to the chapter content for the full framework.

## Activity 3: Alignment Audit

**Time:** 25 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** Large sheets of paper (A3 or flip chart) and markers, or a shared digital whiteboard

### Instructions

1. Each group chooses one real training session that a member has designed or is designing. The person whose session it is briefly explains the objectives (two minutes).
2. On the large sheet, the group draws three columns: **Objectives**, **Activities**, **Assessments**. They fill in what currently exists for that session.
3. The group draws lines connecting each activity and assessment to the objective it serves (five minutes). They look for three things:
   - Objectives with no matching activity or assessment (gaps).
   - Activities or assessments that do not connect to any objective (extras).
   - Cases where the verb in the objective does not match what the activity actually asks learners to do (mismatches) — for example, an objective that says "design" but an activity that only asks learners to "list."
4. The group spends five minutes proposing concrete fixes: one activity to add, one to cut, or one to redesign.
5. Gallery walk (eight minutes). Groups leave their sheets on the tables and rotate to view other groups' audits. Each visiting group leaves one sticky note with a suggestion or observation.

### Facilitator notes

The gallery walk is where the real learning happens — seeing other people's alignment problems makes it easier to spot your own. During the walk, circulate and listen for recurring patterns. In the debrief, highlight any themes you noticed: "Three groups had objectives pitched at 'apply' but activities that only reached 'remember' — what does that tell us?" If groups struggle to identify mismatches, ask them to read the objective verb aloud and then describe what the activity actually requires learners to do. The gap usually becomes obvious.

## Activity 4: Sequence Scramble

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Small groups of 3–4
**Materials:** One set of 8–10 topic cards per group (prepare in advance — each card has a concept or skill from a realistic training session, with brief descriptions)

### Instructions

1. Give each group a shuffled set of topic cards. These should come from a plausible training scenario — for example, a short course on "Designing a Research Data Management Plan" with cards like: *"What is metadata"*, *"Choosing a repository"*, *"Funder requirements"*, *"Hands-on: draft a DMP"*, *"File naming conventions"*, *"Why data management matters"*, *"Sharing sensitive data"*, *"Peer review of DMP drafts"*.
2. Groups have eight minutes to arrange the cards in the order they would teach them. They should be ready to explain *why* they chose that sequence — what depends on what, and where learners need a foundation before moving on.
3. Now introduce a twist. Ask each group to pick one concept from their sequence and redesign the order so that concept is taught through productive failure — the challenge comes *before* the explanation. They rearrange the relevant cards and note what framing or support they would add so learners know the struggle is intentional.
4. Two groups share their sequences with the room (two minutes each). The rest of the room responds: "Is there a dependency they missed? Would you sequence anything differently?"

### Facilitator notes

Prepare the topic cards around a domain your participants will recognise — the closer it is to their actual work, the better the discussion. If you have a mixed audience, choose a broadly accessible topic. The productive failure twist often generates disagreement, which is good. Some participants will feel strongly that foundations must always come first; others will see the value in letting learners discover gaps. Use this tension in the debrief: "Both approaches work — the question is *when* each one is the better fit. What would help you decide?"

## Activity 5: The Cutting Room Floor

**Time:** 15 minutes
**Group size:** Pairs
**Materials:** None (participants use their own session plans or outlines — on paper or laptop)

### Instructions

1. Each participant pulls up or sketches an outline for a training session they are working on. If they do not have one handy, they can use any session they have delivered before.
2. Working alone for three minutes, each person identifies one item in their session — a topic, a slide, an activity — that they suspect does not directly serve a learning objective but they have been reluctant to cut. They write it on a sticky note or scrap of paper.
3. Pairs swap their "candidates for cutting." Each person plays the role of an honest colleague: they read the item, ask the author to explain why it is there, and then give a straight recommendation — keep, cut, or move to a supplementary resource. They have five minutes for this conversation.
4. Debrief as a whole group (five minutes). Ask: "Who decided to cut something? What made it hard? Who decided to keep something after all — and what justified it?"

### Facilitator notes

This activity works because it externalises a decision that trainers usually agonise over in private. Having someone else say "I think you can cut this" is often the permission people need. Watch for participants who insist everything in their session is essential — gently prompt them: "If you had to lose fifteen minutes from this session, what would go?" The constraint forces a choice. Emphasise that cutting does not mean the content is bad — it means it does not belong *here*.

## Activity 6: Pitfall Clinic

**Time:** 20 minutes
**Group size:** Whole group, then pairs
**Materials:** Projector or whiteboard

### Instructions

1. Display the list of common pitfalls from the chapter (five items — designing around your interests, confusing coverage with learning, front-loading theory, designing in isolation, assuming one format fits all). Read through them briefly — one sentence each, no long explanations.
2. Ask each participant to silently pick the one pitfall they are most guilty of in their own training design. Give them one minute to think.
3. Participants find a partner who chose a *different* pitfall (they announce their choice by holding up one to five fingers corresponding to the list). Pairs have eight minutes to do the following:
   - Describe a specific moment where that pitfall showed up in their own work.
   - Brainstorm one concrete change they could make to their current session to address it.
4. Whole-group debrief (five minutes). Ask for a show of hands: which pitfall was the most common? Invite two or three participants to share the concrete change they identified.

### Facilitator notes

"Front-loading theory before practice" and "confusing coverage with learning" tend to be the most popular choices. If nearly everyone picks the same one, that is worth naming: "This tells us something about how most of us were trained ourselves — we default to what we experienced as learners." The finger-matching step keeps energy up and gets people moving. If the group is small (under ten), skip the matching step and let people pair up freely.
